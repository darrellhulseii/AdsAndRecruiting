Key takeaways
01

Systems over motivation

Consistency in daily actions, like knocking 120 doors, creates a compound effect that outperforms occasional high-effort days.

02

Data is your guide

Track KPIs (doors, conversations, closes) to spot weaknesses, like a close rate dropping from 13% to 7%, and set realistic goals.

03

Own your results

Take responsibility and adapt to challenges instead of blaming outside factors. Ownership unlocks freedom and control.

The seven laws
01Law of Clarity
You can't hit a target you can't see. Success requires a clear vision of your goals and the person you need to become to achieve them.
  • Write goals down: use the "I am so happy and grateful now that..." framework to build emotional investment.
  • Make goals visible: vision boards, screensavers, or daily rewrites keep your purpose top of mind.
  • Reverse-engineer: define the specific actions and habits required to reach your goal.
02Law of Consistency
Small, consistent actions create a compound effect that outperforms occasional, high-effort days.
  • Build systems: rely on daily habits (knocking 100 doors) to remove emotion from performance and avoid "falling to the level of your systems."
  • Focus on lead indicators: prioritize daily actions over lag indicators (results) to build momentum.
  • Follow the formula: competence (consistent action) → confidence (predictable results) → more consistency.
03Law of Averages
Consistent action reveals predictable ratios. Tracking these KPIs gives you objective data to guide improvement.
  • Track KPIs daily: doors knocked, conversations, closes, installs.
  • Use data for pattern recognition: spot weaknesses (a close rate drop from 13% to 7%) and decide objectively where to train.
  • Set data-driven goals: use your KPIs to calculate the activity (doors knocked) needed to hit a specific deal target.
04Law of Intentionality
Success is the result of intentional planning, not accident.
  • Plan your entire day: schedule workouts, meals, and field strategy in advance.
  • Map your turf: know your starting point and route before you begin knocking.
  • Prepare for each door: observe the home to tailor your pitch and be fully present with the customer.
05Law of Adaptability
Your ability to adapt to challenges determines your success. A plan is only useful until you face the unexpected.
  • Anticipate challenges: research your turf, but be ready for inaccurate data, like unexpected competitors.
  • Flip negatives into levers: use objections as opportunities to differentiate your service.
  • Stay positive: focus on what you control (energy, attitude) and use available resources (trainings, managers) to find solutions.
06Law of Ownership
Taking ownership of problems is the only way to solve them.
  • Take personal responsibility: focus on solutions instead of assigning blame.
  • Be accountable: recognize you are in control of your results.
  • Reject victimhood: it is a barrier to improvement and a common reason for termination.
07Law of the Lid
Your self-belief and leadership skills set a ceiling, a "lid," on your potential and your team's effectiveness.
  • Identify your current lid: audit your beliefs and skills to understand your ceiling.
  • Raise your lid intentionally: close the gap to your next level through training, skill development, and personal growth.
  • Embrace continuous improvement: raising your lid is an ongoing process, never a one-time fix.

Operator playbook

Advanced paid plays

How the best paid marketers — Gary Vaynerchuk, Alex Hormozi, and the current Meta playbook — actually run ads now. Same compliance rules as the rest of the plan.

Creative is the new targeting Gary Vee + Meta
The creative, not the audience, now decides who you reach and whether it works. Feed broad targeting and Meta's AI a high volume of strong creative and let it find the buyers.
Run it: run 10-15 short vertical clips per campaign on broad targeting; let the algorithm sort it; refresh by swapping just the first three seconds.
Day trading attention Gary Vaynerchuk
Treat paid like a trading desk: fast creative testing, daily measurement, and cultural timing. If an ad flops, it is almost always the creative, not the platform.
Run it: launch small, read the numbers within days, kill losers fast, and move budget to winners every week.
Volume and thumb-stopping hooks Gary Vaynerchuk
Make enough creative, edit it native to each platform, and win the first three seconds or lose the view. One idea per ad.
Run it: batch-film at the door, cut many hooks from one shoot, and test a new opening line against the same video body.
The grand slam offer Alex Hormozi
Make the offer so good people feel stupid saying no: stack real value, add a guarantee, add a reason to act now. A great offer beats a clever ad.
Run it: lead with a genuinely strong offer — free availability and speed check, free home-security assessment, or an install perk — not just "get internet."
Lead magnets that convert Alex Hormozi
Give away something valuable that solves one narrow problem and points to your core offer. Make it good enough to charge for, and build several versions to rotate.
Run it: offer a free address speed-and-availability report, a home-security checklist, or an ISP bill audit, captured through your consented form.
Prove it, then pour fuel Alex Hormozi
There are only four ways to get leads: warm outreach, cold outreach, content, and paid ads. Prove the offer and angle with organic first, then scale the winner with paid.
Run it: find the hook that already performs in your organic clips, then put ad budget behind that exact angle.
Consolidate, do not fragment Meta 2026
The algorithm needs data to learn, so one larger campaign beats five tiny ones. Keep the structure simple: one prospecting campaign plus one retargeting campaign.
Run it: put all your best creative in a single broad prospecting campaign rather than splitting budget across many ad sets.
Retarget warm audiences Meta 2026
People who already watched your videos or visited the site convert far cheaper than cold traffic. Always run a dedicated retargeting layer.
Run it: send 15-20% of budget to video viewers and site visitors from the last 7-30 days with a direct "check your address" ad.
Low-friction lead forms Meta 2026
Instant lead forms cut fill time from minutes to seconds and generate more leads, while Pixel and Conversions API feed the signal back so delivery improves.
Run it: use instant forms that keep your consent box and privacy policy, sync leads to your inbox instantly, and respond within five minutes.

Step by step, platform by platform
Meta (Facebook & Instagram)Best for local volume
  1. In Meta Ads Manager, create a campaign and choose the Leads objective (collect applicants with an instant form) or Traffic (send them to an application page).
  2. Critical: when asked "is your ad about credit, employment, housing, or social issues?" select Employment. This is required for hiring ads and locks your targeting.
  3. Set location to your service area by city or region — ZIP codes are not allowed, and Meta auto-expands to a 15-mile radius. Leave age at 18-65+ and all genders.
  4. Skip lookalikes and detailed interest targeting (unavailable for this category). Lean on broad reach and strong creative to find the right people.
  5. Build a short vertical video or image. Lead with the opportunity: flexible schedule, weekly pay potential, paid training, no experience needed. Keep claims honest.
  6. If using a lead form, ask only for name, phone, email, and availability — you cannot collect age, gender, or ZIP. Keep your consent line and privacy link.
  7. Start at $20-40/day, optimize for leads, and review applicants daily. Respond within minutes while interest is hot.
Budget: Roughly $20-40/day to start. You pay per lead; cost per applicant varies by market.
TikTokBest for younger reps
  1. Create a TikTok Ads Manager account and a new campaign; choose the Lead Generation objective.
  2. Pick an Instant Form (native and fastest) or send traffic to your application page.
  3. Target your area by location. TikTok skews younger, which suits entry-level door-to-door roles. Follow non-discrimination rules and avoid targeting protected traits.
  4. Make native, authentic vertical video — a rep day-in-the-life, a "how I started knocking doors" POV, with a fast hook in the first two seconds. Polished corporate ads underperform here.
  5. Build the lead form (name, phone, email, availability) with your consent line. Keep claims truthful, no income guarantees.
  6. Start at $20-50/day, let it run a few days to learn, then scale the winning creative.
Budget: Around $20-50/day. Native UGC-style video far outperforms anything that looks like an ad.
IndeedBest for steady applicant flow
  1. Create a free Indeed employer account and post the role (for example, "Door-to-door sales rep — 1099, commission"). Be clear it is independent-contractor and commission-based.
  2. Add screener questions and deal-breakers (reliable transportation, comfortable with commission, weekend availability) so unqualified applicants are filtered out.
  3. Free posts get buried within a day, so Sponsor the job to keep it visible. Sponsored Jobs run a pay-per-click auction: set a daily or monthly budget and pay when seekers click or start an application.
  4. Indeed suggests a budget; the floor is $5/day, but each sponsored posting needs at least $25/day to run. Effective cost per application usually lands around $15-50 by role and market.
  5. Review applicants daily, message strong ones immediately, and pause the sponsorship anytime once you have enough.
Budget: Free to post; $25/day minimum to sponsor. Plan on roughly $15-50 per application.
ZipRecruiterBest for wide distribution
  1. Create an employer account. ZipRecruiter is subscription-based per job slot with a short 2-4 day free trial that requires a card and auto-converts — get cancellation terms in writing first.
  2. Post the job; ZipRecruiter distributes it to 100+ boards and uses AI matching to surface candidates.
  3. Use "Invite to apply" to proactively reach matched candidates instead of waiting for applications.
  4. Add screening questions. A TrafficBoost add-on can push a posting higher in results but costs extra.
  5. Pricing is not public (custom quotes, roughly $299-899/mo per slot). Ask for a small-business discount and confirm exactly what is included before subscribing.
Budget: Subscription, about $299+/mo per job slot. Best when you are hiring continuously.
Google SearchBest for high-intent seekers
  1. In Google Ads, create a Search campaign and accept the employment policy acknowledgment Google shows for hiring ads.
  2. Target your area by city or radius (minimum about 1 km). ZIP-code targeting and age, gender, parental, or marital targeting are blocked for employment ads.
  3. Bid on intent keywords: "sales jobs near me," "door to door sales jobs," "commission sales rep [city]," "1099 sales jobs."
  4. Add negative keywords to cut waste, such as "remote" or "work from home" if those do not apply.
  5. Write ads that lead with the opportunity and location, and send clicks to a simple application or lead page. Track form submissions as conversions.
  6. Also list the role free on Google for Jobs by posting it on your site or a connected board — it appears in search at no cost.
Budget: Pay per click on hiring keywords. Pair the paid search with the free Google for Jobs listing.
LinkedInBest for warm reach
  1. Post the role free on LinkedIn (one free listing at a time, visible about 14 days) and share it to your network and any local groups.
  2. To reach more people, promote the post — it runs a pay-per-click auction. Set a daily or total budget (minimum about $7-10/day) and you only pay for clicks from candidates.
  3. Expect roughly $1.50-4.50 per click, often a low cost per applicant, though door-to-door sales is less native here than the job boards.
  4. Add screening questions and route applicants to your form or ATS.
  5. Keep your company page active with rep spotlights and culture so warm candidates already know you.
Budget: Free to post; promote from about $7-10/day on a pay-per-click model.
SnapchatBest for Gen Z reach
  1. Create a Snapchat Ads account in Ads Manager and a new campaign; choose Lead Generation, or Traffic to your application page.
  2. Use a Snap lead form or send clicks to your form. Target your area — Snapchat skews very young, good for entry-level field roles.
  3. Shoot full-screen, sound-on, native vertical video — fast and authentic, a rep talking to camera.
  4. Follow non-discrimination rules and keep claims honest, with no income guarantees.
  5. Start small at $15-30/day and scale the creative that earns the cheapest leads.
Budget: Around $15-30/day. Reaches the youngest field-sales candidates.
CraigslistBest for cheap local
  1. Go to the Craigslist page for your metro, choose "create a posting," then "job offered."
  2. Pick the jobs category. US posts are paid, roughly $10-75 depending on city (San Francisco runs highest); there is no free jobs option in the US.
  3. Use a plain, searchable title like "Sales rep — door to door — [city]" rather than a clever one; clear titles get more responses.
  4. Describe the 1099 commission role honestly and add a simple apply method (your form or email).
  5. Posts run about 30 days, then you repost and pay again. You can also browse the free resume section for your city.
Budget: About $10-75 per 30-day post by metro. Cheap, local, blue-collar reach.
NextdoorBest for hyperlocal trust
  1. Create a Nextdoor business page; you can post your opening to local neighborhoods organically for free.
  2. For more reach, run Nextdoor local ads targeted to the neighborhoods you canvass.
  3. Lean into the local angle — hiring neighbors for local field work — since Nextdoor users trust nearby businesses.
  4. Route interested neighbors to your form and respond fast and personally.
  5. Pair it with real local presence (yard signs, community boards) for compounding trust.
Budget: Free organic posts; paid local ads optional. Strong neighborhood trust.

The rule that makes this work. Claude generates and formats the content; an official-API scheduler publishes it on a timer. Tools that log in and type your posts for you violate platform terms and risk your brand accounts — so we never use them.

Generate

Claude produces a batch of posts or article links in your voice, compliant, as a CSV ready to import.

Schedule

Refill

When the queue runs low, ask Claude for the next batch and re-import. The cycle repeats.

Alan Weiss — the consulting marketing playbook
Value-based fees Pricing
Price the outcome you create, never your time or hours. Raise the perceived value instead of lowering the fee, and charge for the client's improved future, not your deliverables.

Attract buyers to you with a steady stream of content and thought leadership instead of chasing them. Become the obvious object of interest in your area.
Run it: your organic content engine is market gravity — keep it running daily.
The accelerant curve Offers
Offer easy, low-barrier entry points that pull people toward higher-value, higher-trust relationships, ending in unique offers only you have.
Run it: free availability check first, then service, then referrals and add-ons.
The economic buyer Access
Reach the one person who can authorize the decision without anyone else's approval, and agree up front on the objective and the value.
Run it: at the door, talk to the decision-maker, not just whoever answers.
Referrals first Growth
A peer endorsing a peer is the highest-quality marketing there is. Build a body of work and let satisfied customers spread it for you.
Run it: ask every happy customer for two neighbors who'd want the same.

What the giants did to win
BlackRock Platform + scale
Put risk management at the core, built a proprietary platform (Aladdin) that competitors now depend on, and scaled through bold acquisitions — buying the iShares ETF business in the 2009 crisis made it the largest asset manager in the world.
Lesson: own something hard to copy; move decisively when others can't.
Blackstone Reputation + focus
Leveraged reputation and relationships to raise a record first fund, then scaled — while deliberately expanding only into areas where it had a real edge, protecting its reputation.
Lesson: trust opens doors; stay in your lane of advantage.
Berkshire Hathaway Moats + patience
"A moat that must be continuously rebuilt will eventually be no moat at all." — Warren Buffett
Buy and build durable economic moats — brand and pricing power, switching costs, network effects, cost advantages from scale — allocate capital patiently, and think in decades.
Lesson: a real, durable advantage compounds; chase durability, not hype.
Enduring greatness Fortune 500 research
Across the companies that lasted: humble-but-driven leaders, the right people first, brutal honesty about reality, and focus on the one thing you can be best at — run by a culture of discipline. Fortune-scale staples: clear vision, constant innovation, and keeping top talent.
Lesson: endurance depends more on what you do to yourself than on the market.
